Egan Vickers

My name is Egan Vickers, and I am a second year GIMM student who has been working to improve my skills in a variety of fields relating to coding, design, and as a teammate. I have ambitions to learn about a variety of fields relating to sound, including sound design and foley. While this may be my primary target for learning, I strive to be proficient in all fields in order to be helpful in all ways with those I work with. In the following parts of this page, I will discuss and show different parts of my career so far in what I've worked on and thoughts of the time on why I made certain design decisions.

Since I was young, I've always had a love of sound design and music. This was prominent in my love for movies and games, in which I would seek out specific sounds and scores in media for my own entertainment. When I entered schooling, I really got into music and spent 8 years as a percussionist. Percussion really spoke to me as it was a combination of traditional musical instruments, but also various objects that you would use to make unconventional music with.

Artifacts

When I started in GIMM, I had no experience with any form of coding or design. This was frustrating at the start, given that most the projects we worked on required content that wasn't necessarily taught during lectures. This led to me figuring out different solutions to problems in unconventional or inefficient ways due to my lack of knowledge. The best example of this is my first individual game project I worked on, Nebula.

Nebula

This was the first game I created by myself, and your objective was to survive as long as you could in an infinite hallway while matching the colors of the walls that came towards you. It was a simple concept, and largely took inspiration from games like Jetpack Joyride. Looking back, there was so many ways I could've improved the coding as I used hexcodes in the code to match the player's color and the walls color (allowing you to pass through), but I'm still happy with the way it came out.

Comic

Another example I'll use is one of the projects I worked on with in a group of four people (including me). The details of this project was the we needed to make a interactive comic book game, using the panels of the comic to transfer into different levels. In addition, we were given a concept that the game needed to be based around. With this is mind, this was quite the fun project to work on. One large learning experience that I took away from this was that ambition is good, but it must be tempered by deadlines and realistic outcomes. This was significant as this project went through many iterations and design ideas, most of which I worked with a fellow classmate to design. Eventually, I had to step up into a semi-leadership position, giving deadlines and directions for what to work on. For myself personally, I worked on multiple parts of this project, such as parts of the art, level design, core mechanics, and overview.

Maze

This is the final example I'll give, and is one of my favorite projects that I worked on. This game is a multiplayer party game with AR systems in place. In the image to the right, you can see the general layout of the maze that we made, although that photo was from a prototype version of this game. The game was simple, and only needed a deck of 52 cards to work. The camera would scan the card you present it, using the number for how many squares and the suite for what direction you go. I helped primarily on the maze layout and designing/coding traps and the games concept itself. This was a great learning experience as the project was complicated, fun to concept, and filled with problems both in the game and in our work group. With those problems between certain members of our group, it helped me learn how to work with problematic co-workers and resolve issues between them in the interest of seeing the project finished.
